Introduction


Revolutionizing Social Media Analytics:
How Machine Learning is Transforming the Way Brands Connect with Consumers.


The use of Machine Learning (ML) in social media has become a hot topic in recent years, with institutional entities exploring the ways in which these technologies can help them better understand, segment, and organize the vast amounts of data they collect daily. The ethical as well as unethical use of social media analytics has helped steer the common public towards estimated paths in recent years. Even though there is uncertainty surrounding AI and machine learning after new global data privacy legislation, its potential benefits cannot be underestimated and more importantly it has never been more significant.

The late 2022 period saw a spike in usage of Twitter hashtags “CFBR”, “laid off”, “jobsearch”, and similar horrific sounding phrases. Public sentiment erupted due to a combination of world-strucks such as the Covid-19, the Russian War, and then, when the world appeared to be on the verge of a silver lining, the recession blanketed the world, especially the US market. In the midst of all the chaos, Apple successfully launched its latest series of iPhones, iPads and Macbooks. Financial institutions such as Morgan Stanley and Capital One reported quarterly profits and continued their hiring efforts, while controversial players with their emotionally charged reputations like Goldman Sachs struggled with faltering attempts to develop a stream of steady, repeatable revenue in stark contrast to its rival’s resilience in asset management.


A critical question arises as to how they could be sure it wouldn't turn out to be a critical misstep?

According to Bloomberg's interview with Apple's Supply and Delivery Chain Lead in July 2022, it was confirmed that the iPhone 14 supply chain was not impacted by geopolitical tensions or financial concerns. This was determined through sentiment trend analysis and engagement-reach charts, ensuring the launch event would not be delayed. Morgan Stanley and Capital One's human-centered approach, rational client management, and empathetic dealings with employees might have safeguarded them from suffering losses. These occurrences raise the need for thoughtful reflection on the ethical utilization of social media data from sources such as Twitter and Facebook.

Twitter, a microblogging platform, has repeatedly been shown to be an effective communication tool for both corporations and governments. It has become increasingly important for businesses to engage with their customers, while governments are able to connect with the public on a more personal level. As a result, the data generated on Twitter has become a valuable resource for organizations looking to make informed decisions and improve their performance.

By utilizing Twitter data, organizations, governments, and NGOs can gain a greater understanding of public reactions, engagement, and anticipate the impact of future marketing campaigns, thereby allowing them to identify new opportunities for growth and success.


The objective of this project is to design and execute a comprehensive solution for utilizing Twitter data in the domains of customer service, influencer identification, and event tracking. The solution encompasses the entire process, from pipeline creation, data gathering, to automated deployment for user interaction. The project endeavors to create a dynamic dashboard that integrates and visualizes relevant Twitter data in a generic form. This aggregated information provides real-time insights into critical metrics, including sentiment analysis, reach identification, engagement segmentation, and location-specific trending terms such as related to University of Colorado Boulder (CUB). The interactive dashboard will give a complete overview of the students' experiences, including things to observe, opinions on new policies, expressions of gratitude, and response to alerts, sporting reactions, making it simpler to understand and take action based on important insights.


In summary, the project aims to aid CUB in making informed decisions, measuring campaign effectiveness by providing a rich insightful representation of students and staffs response. Also the project would try to answer the following questions:

1). What ethical issues have arisen with the use of social media analytics?

2). What were the burning issues in CU Boulder trending on Twitter in 2022-23?

3). What all kind of information could the dynamic dashboard provide?

4). Can the dashboard provide real-time insights?

5). What metrics will be included in the sentiment analysis?

6). How will the dashboard provide location-specific trending terms?

7). How will the project measure the effectiveness of campaigns at the CU Boulder?

8). What type of information will the project provide about CU Boulder's students and staff?

9). What role does sentiment trend analysis play in ensuring product launches are not delayed?

10). How does the dashboard provide a complete overview of the experiences of students at CU Boulder?

Twitter is a popular social media platform that is widely used for communication, information sharing and entertainment. As a result, there is a significant amount of data generated on the platform every day. Methods of Data Gathering:

1. Twitter API (discontinued from Feb 1, 2023): Using a developer account the Twitter API provides a way to access Twitter data in a programmatic way.
2. Scraping: Web scraping is another method of data gathering, and this project uses one such tool, snscrape. This project utilizes a wrapper around this tool to extract thousands of tweets in an iterative manner.
3. Third-party tools: There are several third-party tools such as Hootsuite, TweetDeck, and Socialbakers that can be used to gather data from Twitter.

The wrapper code used to extract twitter data using the API tool could be found here.

The process of collecting data began with a review of the literature and examination of various sources, including the developer API and Kaggle. However, since the scope was limited to the University of Colorado Boulder, specific Twitter metadata was required. To address this, the snscrape tool was utilized. SNScrape API accesses the archived Twitter repository through asynchronous ten-fold developer accounts obtained over time. It provides a powerful search query that can be passed to the endpoint call, but the user does not need any API key. The returned JSON object contains several meta-data information that could be mined.

For the purposes of the project, a wrapper was created to make data retrieval easier, returning only the necessary attributes in a list form. The request body that could be used for the original endpoint looked like:

"{search_query} since:{YYYY-MM-DD} until:{YYYY-MM-DD}"

Example of a sample POST request made via the original tool:

										import os
	import pandas as pd
	#!pip install git+https://github.com/JustAnotherArchivist/snscrape.git
	import snscrape.modules.twitter as sntwitter # the magic
	# API POST message body
	search_query = "cuboulder"
	start_date = "2022-12-01"
	end_date = "2022-12-03"
	message_body = '{} since:{} until:{}'.format(search_query, start_date, end_date)
	print(message_body)
	# returns a generator with json response
	historical_metadata_raw = sntwitter.TwitterSearchScraper(message_body).get_items()
	historical_metadata = [tweet for tweet in historical_metadata_raw]

OVERVIEW

Principal components analysis (PCA) refers to the process by which principal components are computed, and the subsequent use of these components in understanding the data. 

PCA is an unsupervised approach, since it involves only a set of features 𝑋1,𝑋2,...,𝑋𝑝, and no associated response 𝑌.

When there are large number of features which may be correlated with each other (pair-plot), principal components analysis (PCA) allows us to summarize this set with a smaller number of representative variables that collectively explain most of the variability in the original set.

Apart from producing derived variables for use in supervised learning problems, PCA also serves as a tool for data visualization (visualization of the observations or visualization of the variables). It can also be used as a tool for data imputation — that is, for filling in missing values in a data matrix.

What is Clustering ?

Clustering is an unsupervised machine learning technique, which partitions the entire dataset into a fixed number of clusters or groups in such a way that observations in each group exhibit similar behavior or characteristics and are homogeneous. The observations in the same group are more similar than the observations in other groups.


What is Clustering ?

Clustering is an unsupervised machine learning technique, which partitions the entire dataset into a fixed number of clusters or groups in such a way that observations in each group exhibit similar behavior or characteristics and are homogeneous. The observations in the same group are more similar than the observations in other groups.



Clustering Algorithms:

Based on the type of clustering algorithm used, there are different types of clustering methods:


    - Hierarchical clustering: It is a tree based clustering method where the observations are divided into a tree like structure using distance as a measure.

    - Centroid based clustering: In this method, the observations are partitioned into a predefined number of clusters (k) such that within cluster variances is minimum. k-means clustering is the most commonly used centroid based clustering algorithm.

    - Density-based clustering: In this method, the observations are grouped together based on density of points that are closely packed. DBSCAN and OPTICS are two popular density-based algorithms.

    - Distribution-based clustering: In this method, the observations are grouped based on the distribution models and observations in the same cluster are most likely coming from the same distribution. Gaussian mixture algorithm is an example of Distribution-based clustering.

Vectorization

The pre-processed dataset consists of pre-processed tweets in English language. These tweets needs to be vectorized into numbers.There are various vectorization engines available in the market, such as:

  • Tf-idf: Used for small corpus where the vocabulary can be contained.
  • class-Tf-idf: Enhanced tf-idf trained on Transformer encoder-decoder network, based on class-topics.
  • Word2Vec: Good for starting with embeddings in smaller text sentences.
  • Doc2Vec: Great option for vectorizing texts spanning across paragraphs.
  • Glove/Google/Stanfordnlp: Pretrained models for beginners.
  • Spacy: Industry applied and one of the most used quick-vectorizers.
  • Universal Sentence Encoder/FastAI: Google and Facebook trained embedders.
  • Transformers: State-of-the-art embedding networks, based on encoder-decoder architecture.
  • Fine-tuned with domain data, Roberta, Sentence-Bert, CardiffNLP are some of the most widely used deep learning embedders.

This project uses "Distilled-Roberta-Version1" which has been fine-tuned on twitter-data by cardiffnlp. It's a fine-tuned pre-trained model based on original Roberta. Each vector consists of 768 high-space dimensions. It could be found: here

Context

- An $embedding$ is a numerical representation of words, phrases, or sentences that captures the meaning and context (both semantic and syntactic) of the text in a high-dimensional space.

- In the case of a document (one or more sentences), an $embedding$ represents the meaning of these sentences in a way that can be processed by machine learning models.

- A $Transformer$ model is a type of neural network that is commonly used for natural language processing. One of the main features of the Transformer model is its ability to generate word embeddings that capture the meaning and context of a sentence. These embeddings are generated by processing the sentence through the layers of the Transformer model.

Project Embeddings

- In our dataset (tweets) each sentence have been vectorized into a vector of [1 x 768] dimensions.

- When we say that a text sentence is embedded into $768 \space dimensions$ by a pre-trained Transformer model, we mean that the sentence is **now represented as a vector of 768 numbers**, where each number in the vector corresponds to a different dimension in the embedding space.

- These dimensions were learned by the Transformer model during training and represent different aspects of the meaning and context of the sentence.

- For example, one dimension of the embedding vector might correspond to the presence of a certain topic or concept in the sentence, while another dimension might correspond to the sentiment or emotional tone of the sentence. By combining these different dimensions in a high-dimensional space, the Transformer model is able to capture the complex and nuanced meaning of a sentence.

- In *extremely* simple words, text = "Coach Prime era has begun!" go now coach prime have has era begin end ....] word_vector = [0 0 1 1 0 1 1 1 0 ....] => One-Hot-Encoding topics = start end sentiment complexity presence_of_Adjective_followed_by_a_Noun ... embedding = [0.38 0 0.56 -0.32 0.87 0 ] = a meaningful vector representing meaning & context of the sentence in 768 numbers.
